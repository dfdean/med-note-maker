Chest Pain or NSTEMI      

    Angina

    • Unstable Angina = Transient ischemia, normal(-ish) troponins
    • NSTEMI = subendocardial ischemia with +Troponins. Partially occluded coronary
    • STEMI = Transmural myocardial ischemia, +Troponins. Fully occluded coronary

    NSTEMI Types

    • Type 1 - Plaque rupture
    • Type 2 - Supply-Demand Mismatch
    • Type 3 - Cardiac death
    • Type 4 - Peri-PCI
    • Type 5 - Peri-CABG

    RV Infarction

    Signs/Symptoms:

    • ST-segment elevation in V1 or V4R (right-sided V4)
    • hypotension
    • clear lung fields
    • jugular venous distention

    Treat:

    • Aggressive volume resuscitation to optimize right ventricular filling (preload)
    • intravenous vasopressors if hypotension persists
    • urgent coronary revascularization
    • AVOID Nitrates, dobutamine

    Imaging

    • Exercise EKG - initial test in patients with intermediate probability of coronary artery disease and normal findings on baseline ECG. Cannot use if: If there are baseline ECG abnormalities (like LBBB), inability to exercise
    • Myocardial perfusion imaging (MPI) (Nuclear medicine stress testing). Looks for differences in blood flow to detect ischemia. Less contrast goes to areas supplied by coronary vessels with stenosis
    • Cardiac magnetic resonance (CMR) imaging
    • Adenosine myocardial perfusion imaging. UNLESS asthma or reactive airways disease
    • Coronary artery calcium (CAC) scoring cannot determine the degree of obstruction. It has no use in patients on a statin

    High Sensitivity Trop

    • High Sens troponin delta is less than 10-20%, which is less consistent with ACS
    • At my institution (Kentucky, USA), call Cardiology for high sensitivity Troponin over 150 or if delta high sensitivity Troponin exceeds 10-20 percent

    Misc

    • Contraindications to nitroglycerin: Sildenafil or other erectile dysfunction med in past 7 days, Bradycardia (HR below 60), Hypotension, R-side or inferior wall MI
    • Do not anticoagulate in a Type-2 NSTEMI caused by Hypertensive Urgency, as it increases risk of hemorrhagic stroke
    • ASCVD score http://tools.cardiosource.org/ASCVD-Risk-Estimator/
    • Low evidence to hold PPI if on Clopidogrel (Maybe worse with Omeprazole, but still no stong indication to hold)

Cirrhosis/ACLD          
MELD:      Cr  TBili  Na  INR  Albumin           
     
 



AASLD now classifies some disease as "Advanced chronic liver disease (ACLD)", which does NOT require liver biopsy. So, it is treated the same as Cirrhosis, but can be diagnosed without an invasive procedure Ref. ACLD is diagnosed with Liver Stiffness Measurements over 15kPa. (below 10KPa rules ACLD). If Liver Stiffness Measurements are between 10KPa and 15kPa, then if platelet count is below 110 then this is also ACLD Ref. I will use ACLD and Cirrhosis interchangeably since biopsies are very uncommon.

There are two subtypes of ACLD:

  • ACLD without prior decompensation
  • Compensated ACLD (cACLD)
This is significant - median survival for compensated is 12 years, while median survival after one decompensating event is 1.5 years or less Ref. Decompensated ACLD has clinical symptoms of Portal Hypertension, which are:
  • Overt ascites
  • Variceal hemorrhage
  • Overt hepatic encephalopathy
These usually present when portal pressure gradients is 10mm Hg or more.

Portal Hypertension is pressure difference between the portal vein and the inferior vena cava over 5mmHg. There are three types of Portal Hypertension

  • Prehepatic - resistance in the portal vein
  • Intrahepatic - resistance in the liver This can be several types
    • Presinusoidal - Cirrhosis scarring of the sinusoids
    • postsinusoidal - Resistance in the efferent (central) vein
  • Posthepatic - resistance in the IVC

Renal Tubular Acidosis:
  • Type I: Distal, Low serum K, High urine pH. Causes: Obstructive uropathy, Sjögren's, Rheumatoid, Lupus, Sickle cell anemia, Hyperthyroid, Hyperparathyroid, Hepatitis, Primary Biliary Cirrhosis, renal medullary cystic disease, chronic UTI
  • Type II: Proximal, (usually Fanconi in adults). Low serum K, Glucosuria, Phosphaturia, Proteinuria. Causes: Wilson disease, drugs (Tenofovir, ifosfamide, Acetazolamide), Multiple myeloma
  • Type IV: Distal, Low or Normal or High serum K. Causes: Spironolactone or eplerenone, ACE inhibitors or ARBS, Trimethoprim, Pentamidine, NSAIDs, Diabetic nephropathy, HIV/AIDS, Addison's disease, sickle cell disease, urinary tract obstruction, Lupus, Amyloidosis
  • Hyperkalemic RTAs include Type 4 (hypoaldosteronism), and voltage-dependent RTA (defects in distal sodium reabsorption)
  • HypOkalemic RTAs include Type 1 (Distal - defects in hydrogen ion excretion) and type 2 (Proximal - defects in bicarbonate reabsorption in the proximal tubule)

Types of Heart Failure:
HFrEF LVEF 40 or lower
HFmrEF LVEF between 41 and 49
HFpEF LVEF 50 or higher


Medications for HFrEF (LVEF 40 or lower)
  • ARNI or ACE/ARB
  • SGLT2
  • MRA
  • Diuretics
  • ICD
  • Hydralazine
  • Ivabradine (HR 170 at rest)
  • ICD
  • No CCB
  • No PPAR and DDP-4
Medications for HFpEF (LVEF over 40)
  • ARB or ARNi
  • SGLT2
  • MRA (maybe)
LVEF is a spectrum, and among patients with LVEF 41-49, particularly the lower end of this spectrum, seem to respond to HFrEF treatment.

Medications

  • ACE/ARB
    In patients with HFrEF and NYHA II-III symptoms, use ARNi (either start or switch ACE/ARB to ARNi) If switching, wait 36 hours between ACEi and ARNi doses. Patients with HFpEF, ARNi or ARB may be considered (decrease hospitalizations). In HFrEF, can use ACEi, or if intolerant to ACEi then can consider ARB. "An ARNi is recommended as de novo treatment in hospitalized patients with acute HF before discharge given improvement in health status, reduction in the prognostic biomarker NT-proBNP, and improvement of LV remodeling parameters compared with ACEi/ARB."
    Angioedema due to ACEi may be due to reduced degradation of vasoactive peptides bradykinin and substance P. Can switch to ARB, see Rasmussen, Eva, et al, "Angiotensin II receptor blockers are safe in patients with prior angioedema related to angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors – a nationwide registry-based cohort study". Note that ARNi contains Neprilysin inhibitors, but those inhibitors can cause angioedema when used with ACEi and are contraindicated.

  • SGLT2 Inhibitors
    Use for HFrEF and HFpEF, and these reduce hospitalizations and cardiovascular mortality, even in non-Diabetic patients and benefits are independent of the glucose-lowering effects.

  • Beta Blockers
    In HFrEF, beta blockers reduce symptoms but in HFrEF patients with history of MI or acute coronary syndrome (ACS) they also reduce mortality Use bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol succinate.

  • MRA - Mineralcorticoid Receptor Antagonist
    In HFrEF Spironolactone or eplerenone reduces morbidity and mortality, if eGFR is over 30 and potassium is below 5.0 mEq/L. Stop if K cannot be kept below 5.5. In HFpEF, MRAs may be considered but only decrease hospitalizations not mortality.

  • Diuretics
    Other than MRAs, these may not affect mortality, they only improve symptoms. > >

  • Calcium Channel Blockers (CCB)
    Stop nondihydropyridine CCBs (diltiazem and verapamil) in HFrEF. Dihydropyridine CCBs may be used for hypertension

  • PPAR and DDP-4
    Stop Thiazolidinediones (PPAR agonists) and DPP-4 inhibitors (Sitagliptan and others) because they increase hospitalizations.

  • Hydralazine
    African American patients with NYHA class III-IV HFrEF on optimal medical therapy, should also get hydralazine and isosorbide dinitrate

  • Ivabradine
    Patients with symptomatic (NYHA class II to III) HFrEF and LVEF below 35 and sinus rhythm HR ‡70 bpm at rest, should get ivabradine

  • ICD and CRT
    Patients who are at least 40 days post-MI with LVEF below 30 and have reasonable expectation of survival for over 1 year may get an ICD to reduce total mortality. Patients with LVEF below 35%, sinus rhythm, LBBB with QRS over 150ms may also get Cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT). Many patients who meet the indication for CRT also meet indications for ICD



Some Non-Ischemic Causes of HF, both left and right sided
  • Hypertension

  • Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy. It may result from either SVT or VT or frequent PVCs. Time to development of cardiomyopathy varies, but it may develop within days to months of tachycardia onset.

  • Obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS). Daytime hypercapnia with PCO2 greater than 45mm Hg

  • Stress cardiomyopathy (Takotsubo)

  • Cardiac amyloidosis. Increased wall thickness on echo with low voltage on the ECG. Cardiac magnetic resonance (CMR) imaging for diagnosis. There are several causes: light-chain amyloid (AL) amyloidosis and transthyretin amyloid (ATTR) amyloidosis.

  • Peripartum cardiomyopathy. LV dysfunction toward the end of pregnancy or in the months after delivery. Following delivery, add ACE/ARB/ARNi.

  • Sarcoidosis. Cardiac magnetic resonance (CMR) imaging for diagnosis.

  • Infection - HIV, COVID-19, Chagas

  • Drug toxicity - Chemotherapy, Alcohol, Cocaine, Amphetamines

  • Chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension. Diagnose with ventilation-perfusion scan (Chest CTA is less sensitive)

  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM). Autosomal dominant, causes LV Hypertrophy. Avoid beta-blockers, verapamil and refer to ICD

  • Others: hemochromatosis, hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism, acromegaly, Familial cardiomyopathy

Notes on Diuresis (from an old ASN Kidney Week presentation)
  • Adding Acetazolamide helps diuresis by reducing Proximal tubule Na reabsorption and downregulates Pendrin (Cl/HCO23).
  • Diuretic are excreted in PCT by OAT
  • Hypochloremia reduces diuretic response due to less Cl for the NKCC2 to work (the CC2 is 2 Chloride atoms). You can fix this with PO KCl, or Amiloride or Spirono
  • During diuresis, If K remains low add spironolactone at 25 mg PO daily. Also can add Amiloride or Triamterene to blocks K losses. However, Spironolactone does NOT help K losses (ATHENA-HF Trial)
  • SGLT2 DOES help. It blocks PCT reabsorption of Na
  • No evidence coadmin of Albumin helps, and it can cause HTN and Pulm Edema
  • Only add an MRA (spironolactone or eplerenone) if eGFR is over 30 and serum potassium is below 5
  • Loop diuretics may stilumate Renin and so reduce eGFR. Increased urine NaCl loss causes renal pelvic chomoreceptors to activate renal nerve stimulation and reduce GFR. Despite its effects on decreasing Na sensing in Macula Densa, it blunts tubuloglomerular feedback. Loop Diuretics also stimulate vasopressin, so cause free water retention and hyponatremia. However, that also may be outweighed by the free water effects of loop diuretics on the interstitial osmolar gradient.
  • Torsemide may cause Aldo receptor blockade, and maybe decrease Aldo synthesis, decrease myocardial fibrosis, decrease RAAS activation
  • d/c NSAIDs (which blunt sodium excretion)


NT-proBNP Cutoffs (from ICON trial)
  • 450 - Patients under 50 years old
  • 900 - Patients 50-75 years old
  • 1800 - Patients over 75 years old

BNP cutoff is 100 (from "Breathing Not Properly" Trial)

Diagnosing Cardiogenic shock
  • Hypotension (e.g., SBP below 90 mm Hg) Or requirement of vasopressors to maintain systolic BP over 90 mm Hg
  • End-organ hypoperfusion should be present (Decreased mentation, Cold extremities, livedo reticularis, Urine output below 30 mL/h, Lactate over 2 mmol/L)


Misc
  • H2FPEF estimates the liklihood of HFpEF. The E/e ratio is peak early mitral inflow velocity (E) over early diastolic mitral annular velocity (e′). This can estimate LV filling pressure.

  • In women with HF who are pregnant or currently planning for pregnancy, ACEi, ARB, ARNi, MRA, SGLT2i, ivabradine, and vericiguat should not be administered

  • First-line therapy for acute idiopathic pericarditis is aspirin or NSAIDs and colchicine. In patients with recurrent pericarditis initially treated with both colchicine and an NSAID, the addition of a glucocorticoid should be considered.

  • Compression therapy is an essential component of the treatment of venous stasis ulcers Must check ABI since an absolute contraindication to compression therapy is an ABI of 0.5 or less, although cautioun for any patient with an ABI less than 0.9

Hyponatremia            

Na:  Glucose:  Serum Osm   
Urine Osm:  Urine Na:  Urine K: 
 
 
 
 
 
 
  • Usually give IV Furosemide when urine Osm is over 500
  • A loop diuretic will wash out the interstitial gradient and so free water is not reabsorbed even when Aquaporins are open. As a result, a loop diuretic will waste free water (loop diuretic urine is approx 1/2 NS) and so will raise sodium concentration in blood. A Thiazide preserves interstitial gradient so aquaporins reabsorb free water, so Thiazides preserve free water reabsorption and lower sodium concentration.
  • If cirrhotic, then just restrict fluids and diuretics - vaptans are contraindicated
  • Free water clearance = (1 - (urine Osm / serum Osm)) x DailyUrineVolume
  • ElectrolyteFreeWaterClearance = Urine Volume x (1 - ( ( UruneSodium + UrinePotassium ) / SerumSodium ))
    • If FreeWaterClearance over 0 then pt is losing free water and Na will rise
    • If FreeWaterClearance below 0 then pt is retaining free water and Na will drop

GI Bleed            

Hemorrhage Class
  • Class I - No tachycardia and blood pressure is normal, blood loss less than 750mL or 15% total blood volume
  • Class II - Tachycardia (HR over 100) but blood pressure is normal, blood loss 750mL to 1500mL or 15 to 30% total blood volume
  • Class III - Tachycardia (HR over 120) and blood pressure is low, and urine output is decreased, blood loss 1500mL to 2000mL or 30% to 40% total blood volume
  • Class IV - Tachycardia (HR over 120) and blood pressure is low, and urine output is low, blood loss over 2000mL or over 40% total blood volume

Chronic Coronary Disease      

Definition of Very High-Risk

  • History of multiple major ASCVD events
  • OR One major ASCVD event AND two or more high-risk conditions

Major ASCVD Events

  • Recent ACS (within the past 12 mo)
  • History of MI (other than recent ACS events listed above)
  • History of ischemic stroke
  • Symptomatic peripheral artery disease (history of claudication with ABI below 0.85, or previous revascularization or amputation)

High-Risk Conditions

  • Age over 65yo
  • Diabetes
  • Hypertension
  • Chronic kidney disease (any eGFR below 60)
  • Current tobacco smoking
  • History of CABG or PCI
  • LDL-C over 100 mg/dL despite maximal statin
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia
  • History of congestive heart failure

Lipid Treatment

High-intensity statin goal is 50 percent reduction in LDL-C levels. Measure fasting lipids in 4 to 12 weeks after statin initiation or dose adjustment and Q3-12 months thereafter.

  • Statin
  • Ezetimibe - if very high risk max statin with LDL over 70 mg/dL
  • PCSK9 monoclonal antibodies may be used if Statin+Exetimibe are not enough. It binds to PCSK9, a liver enzyme that degrades hepatocyte LDL receptors. Uncertain economic value at US prices
  • Icosapent ethyl may be considered
  • Niacin, or fenofibrate or Omega-3 diet supplements are not beneficial

High intensity

  • Rosuvastatin 20mg

Do not use these for CCD/CAD, but they may be useful for diabetes

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease            

  FEV1:  FVC: 
 
 

COPD is about more than smoking:

  • Tobacco only causes 30-40 percent of all COPD worldwide. Burning wood/dung/coal/crop residues in open stoved cause most.
  • Less than 50 percent of heavy smokers develop COPD.
  • Mow/Middle Income Countries have 85 percent of total COPD burden worldwide
  • Asthma is also a risk for COPD, and asthmatics have 12x higher risk of COPD

Chronic Bronchitis is a condition within COPD, not a form of COPD. It is chronic cough and sputum production for 3 months/year for 2 years, and it affects ~27-35 percent of COPD pts.

COPD seems to be an altered inflammatory response.

  • Both COPD and Asthma are the result of inflammation, but asthma seems to cause more immediate muscle response to an allergen while COPD causes tissue destruction and long term structural changes.
  • COPD starts with macrophages, which bring in other inflammatory cells, then proteases cause destruction of elastin, and eventually leads to peribronchial fibrosis.
  • Airflow obstruction is caused by a mix of small airway disease (increase airway resistance) and parenchymal destruction (reduce elasticity of lung). It also causes loss of alveolar attachments to airways, decrease elastic recoil, which both cause reduced ability to keep the lung open. All of these limit lung emptying during expiration, and so decrease FEV1. This leads to air trapping and hyperinflation.
  • Parenchymal destruction in emphysema also leads to decreased lung diffusing capacity (DLco).
  • In Exacerbations, there is increased inflammation, hich causes worse air trapping, hyperinflation, and reduced expiratory flow.

Diagnosing

  • Symptome: dyspnea, chronic cough, incrreased sputum Spirometry should be repeated if the FEV1/FVC is between 0.6 and 0.8

In spirometry, a Z-score is the number of standard deviations that the raw score is above/below the median score.

Exacerbation Criteria (ROME Criteria)

  • Mild: RR below 24, HR below 95, SaO2 over 92 percent on RA or baseline home O2, CRP below 10
  • Moderate: Must meet at least 3 of these
    • RR over 24
    • HR over 95
    • SaO2 below 92 percent on RA or baseline O2
    • CRP over 10
    • If you get an ABG, then PaO2 must be <= 60 and/or PaCO2 must be >= 45
  • Severe: Must meet at least 3 of these
    • RR over 24
    • HR over 95
    • SaO2 below 92 percent on RA or baseline O2
    • CRP over 10
    • Must have ABG, PaCO2 must be > 45 and pH < 7.35

Severity of Airflow Obstruction - GOLD Staging

  • GOLD1 FEV1 over 0.8
  • GOLD2 FEV1 0.5 - 0.8
  • GOLD3 FEV1 0.3 - 0.5
  • GOLD4 FEV1 below 0.3

Current Symptoms - Modified Medical Research Council Dyspnea Questionaire

  • mMRC0 = dyspnea only with strenuous exercise
  • mMRC1 = dyspnea with rapid walking
  • mMRC2 = dyspnea with normal walking, must walk slowly
  • mMRC3 = Must stop for breath when walking 100 meters
  • mMRC4 = Breathless when dressing or undressing

Other factors for staging COPD

  • Previous Exacerbations - 2 or more exacerbations annually or at least one hospitalization annually
  • Eos Count - This predicts the efficacy of ICS for preventing exacerbations
  • DLco - This measures gast transfer
  • Alpha-1 Antitryptsin

PFT Results:

  • Obstructive Disease ha FEV1/FVC below 70 percent. Air trapping will cause TLC over Upper limit normal.
  • Restrictive Disease has FEV1/FVC is normal or high, and TLC below 80 percent predicted

DLCO measures alveolar surface area, and it distinguishes between disease within alveolar tissue and disease outside the lung.

  • Obstructive Lung Disease:
      Asthma - Normal DLCO COPD - Low DLCO
  • Restrictive Lung Disease:
    • Neuromuscular disorder - Normal DLCO
    • Interstitial Lung Disease - Low DLCO
Lung disease with normal DLCO is like a crumpled up paper sac. The surface area is still there, but it cannot expand.

Treat Stable COPD (Note, there are NO rapid meds)

  • If 0 or 1 exacerbations and no hospitalizations
    • Just a bronchodilator or LABA + LAMA
  • If 2 or more exacerbations or 1 exacerbation leading to hospitalization in the past year:
    • LABA+LAMA - like Vilanterol/Umeclidinium, Formoterol/aclidinuium, Formoterol/glycopyrronium
    • Or LAMA+LABA+ICS if Eos over 300 or if the patient has asthma. Note, do NOT prescribe LABA-ICS alone. You MAY convert LABA+ICS to LABA+LAMA in stable patients
    • If not controlled with LABA + LAMA and Eos > 100 then LAMA+LABA+ICS
  • If not controlled with LABA + LAMA
    • Roflumilast (a PDE4 inhibitor) if FEV1 < 50 percent predicted and chronic bronchitis
    • Azithromycin (in former smokers) 250 to 500 mg 3 times weekly (Ref) or 250 mg once daily

Treat Exacerbations

  • Short Acting Bronchodilators. Start with SABA+SAMA Q1h x2 then Q2-4h.
  • May add LABA+LAMA
  • Steroids (5 days) - Pred 40 x5 days
  • Antibiotics (5 days). Do not use a negative Procal to limit antibiotics as they also have anti-inflammatory effects.
  • BiPAP O2 or Intubation
    • If pH below 7.35 or else PaO2 below 45, then BiPAP
    • If pH below 7.25 or else PaO2 below 40, then Intubation

Prior to Hospital Discharge:

  • Smoking Cessation - Nicotine Gum/patch/lozenge, Varencycline, Buproprion SR,
  • Give O2 if SaO2 < 88 percent or PaO2 < 55mmHg or < 60mmHg AND HCT >= 55 or Pulm HTN)
  • Annual Low Dose CT is recommended in all patients 50-80yo with >20 pack years smoking
  • α1-antitrypsin level should be obtained once in all patients with COPD.
  • Vaccines:
    • Pneumococcal (65yo and up, 19-64 if COPD, Asthma, Smoker, Transplant). Give PCV15 then PPSV23 or else 1 dose of PCV20
    • Covid
    • Flu
    • RSV (age 60yo up)
    • Tdap (if not vaccinated as child)

Things that reduce mortality:
LABA+LAMA+ICS, Stop smoking, Pulm Rehab, O2 therapy, NIVPP, Lung Reduction

Types of Asthma

  • Mild asthma - well controlled with low-intensity treatment such as PRN low-dose ICS-formoterol
  • Moderate asthma - well controlled with Step 3 or Step 4 treatment
  • Severe asthma - remains uncontrolled despite optimized treatment with high-dose ICS-LABA, or that requires high-dose ICS-LABA to prevent it from becoming uncontrolled. This does not include difficulties due inappropriate treatment or non-compliance or obesity

There are many clinical phenotypes of asthma:

  • Allergic asthma: Patients with this asthma phenotype usually respond well to ICS treatment
  • Non-allergic asthma: Patients with non-allergic asthma often demonstrate a lesser short-term response to ICS
  • Cough variant asthma and cough predominant asthma
  • Adult-onset (late-onset) asthma
  • Asthma with persistent airflow limitation: This is thought to be due to airway wall remodeling
  • Asthma with obesity: some obese patients with asthma have prominent respiratory symptoms

Diagnose in adults with positive bronchodilator (BD) responsiveness (reversibility) if FEV1 increases 12-15 percent or more from baseline or FVC increases 200-400 mL with bronchodilator, or if Peak expiratory flow (PEF) increases 20 percent or more. Note that PEF is less reliable than spirometry, but it is better than nothing.

Clinical Frailty Scale
This is a 9-point scale to describe a patients baseline status. It is not a questionnaire, but rather is based on clinical observations.

  • Level 1 - Very Fit - daily vigorous activity. This may include patients on chronic BP meds.
  • Level 2 - Fit - less frequent or less vigorous activity.
  • Level 3 - Managing Well. less frequent or less vigorous activity but some medical symptoms although these do not limit activity.
  • Level 4 – Living with Very Mild Frailty. Some symptoms with activity, such as fatigue. Incomplete symptom control. May require help with heavy housework, or difficulty climbing stairs.
  • Level 5 – Living with Mild Frailty. Does not attempt heavy housework or climbing stairs
  • Level 6 – Living with Moderate Frailty. Dependence for intermediate ADLs, including dependence in bathing.
  • Level 7 – Living with Severe Frailty. Dependence in some ADLs.
  • Level 8 – Living with Very Severe Frailty. Bed-ridden for periods, such as acute illness.
  • Level 9 – Terminally Ill. Independant of activity status

Critical Care:
In the AMA guidelines, "The risk of patient management criteria applies to the patient management decisions made by the reporting physician or other qualified health care professional as part of the reported encounter". So, if an admission includes high risk procedures that require the internist to monitor for adverse effects, then it has high complexity risk.
